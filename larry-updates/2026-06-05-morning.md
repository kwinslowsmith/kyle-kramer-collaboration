## How KRAMOS generates student intelligence

At the start of each semester, every LHUM400 student completes three structured inputs:

1. A Real-World Readiness Audit: scored self-assessment across five professional domains (digital presence, financial foundation, career networking, graduation logistics, professional habits and resilience). 100 points total. Each domain gets a label (Bare / Patchy / Solid / Strong). The audit also produces a Priority Action Items list, rated urgent or important.

2. A class introduction post: background, major, instruments, career goals, post-grad plans, current projects, motivations in their own words.

3. A professional habits post: three habits they want to build, each structured across six sub-questions (goal, current state, strategy, obstacle, approach, timeline) and a full implementation plan.

All three sources go into one YAML frontmatter block per student. The schema has about 40 fields: identity, academic profile, career profile (with raw plus controlled-vocabulary versions of domain tags), readiness audit scores, the full habits structure, growth areas, personality cues, and trio matching tags.

Then three passes:

Pass 1 runs 20 times, once per student. Extracts from the raw data, fills all plain-language `_raw` fields. No invented tags, no inferences beyond what the student wrote.

Pass 2 runs once across the full cohort. Builds a canonical tag taxonomy by collapsing synonyms from all 20 `_raw` fields. Then re-runs all profiles against the approved taxonomy to fill the controlled-vocabulary fields. Every student ends up with the same tag vocabulary, which is what makes Dataview queries work cleanly.

Pass 3 runs once. Takes all re-tagged profiles and proposes student trios. Six principles in priority order: complementary readiness levels, overlapping career interests, shared growth areas, personality balance, background diversity, and at least one higher-readiness student in each group to model behaviors. Each trio proposal comes with a structured rationale and an honest tradeoffs field. Kramer makes the final call.

The whole system lives in Obsidian as typed person entities, each wikilinked to career domain and growth area topic nodes. The graph shows which students share domains, where the cohort's growth gaps cluster, which students are outliers.

## How this connects to Project X

Here is the explicit pipeline I want to put in front of you.

The packet engine needs two inputs: a standard (what the class is working on) and a student profile. The profile fields that matter most are `career_goal_short`, `interests`, `readiness_tier`, `growth_areas`, and `lhum400_goals`.

With those five fields plus a standard, KRAMOS generates a two-page differentiated reader: same learning objective, vocabulary and scenarios drawn from the student's career domain. A film scoring student and a jazz student both read about negotiating freelance contracts, but the film scoring student reads about music licensing agreements and the jazz student reads about sideman fees and touring contracts. One standard, twenty pathways into it.

The `priority_action_items` list from the audit tells us which standards to prioritize per student. A student with urgent gaps in Financial Foundation gets that packet first. A student strong on digital presence but weak on career networking gets the networking reader first. The audit becomes a routing table for the generator.

And then the loop closes the way we sketched it in the June 4 exchange: once your Mastery Tracker has state on each student, that state tells KRAMOS which packet to generate next. The profile is a living document across the semester. The vault updates as Kramer adds observations. The Mastery Tracker updates as students complete work. Project X generates off the current state of both.
